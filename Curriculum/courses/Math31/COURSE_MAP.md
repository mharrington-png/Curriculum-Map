# Middlesex Math Course Overview

## MATH 31

**Course title:** Advanced Algebra

**Status:** Draft normalized from the existing Math 31 course map; requires faculty review.

## 1. Review Content

Review content supports entry into Math 31. Teachers should move efficiently while diagnosing and addressing lingering misconceptions.

- Percentage calculations and percent change
- Exponent properties
- Polynomial vocabulary, multiplying binomials, and simple quadratic solving

## 2. Required Content (Covered Every Semester)

### Unit 1: Linear Functions and Function Notation

- Review and deepen linear-function concepts from prior courses
- Function notation across formulas, graphs, and tables
- Independent and dependent variables
- Average rate of change, interval notation, and total change
- Increasing and decreasing intervals
- Recognizing and interpreting linear models
- Intersections of linear models
- Horizontal, vertical, parallel, and perpendicular lines

### Unit 2: Function Behavior and Structure

- Function definition, notation, and multiple representations
- Domain and range, including algebraic restrictions
- Piecewise functions
- Transformations: shifts, stretches, compressions, and reflections
- Composition of functions
- Inverse functions from formulas, graphs, and tables, including units
- Horizontal-line testing for invertibility
- Graphing inverse functions as reflections across `y = x`
- Concavity described through changing average rates of change

### Unit 3: Quadratic Functions and Equations

- Review and deepen quadratic concepts from prior courses
- Standard, factored, and vertex forms
- Vertex, axis of symmetry, intercepts, zeros, and concavity
- Factoring, the quadratic formula, and completing the square
- Converting among quadratic forms
- Constructing a quadratic from sufficient characteristics
- Quadratic trajectory modeling

### Unit 4: Exponential Functions and Models

- Linear versus exponential change
- Initial value and growth/decay factor
- Finite and continuous compounding
- Finding exponential models from data
- Comparing exponential growth rates
- Exponential domain, range, and asymptotes
- Exponential modeling in context

### Unit 5: Logarithmic Functions and Models

- Logarithms as inverses of exponential functions
- Conversion between logarithmic and exponential forms
- Product, quotient, and power properties
- Solving exponential and logarithmic equations
- Intersections of exponential functions
- Equivalent discrete- and continuous-growth forms
- Doubling time and half-life
- Logarithmic domain, range, and asymptotes

## 3. Extension Material (Optional/Time-Permitting)

- Symmetry and more advanced transformation work
- Absolute-value functions as a transformation application
- Applications of logarithmic scales

## 4. Depth, Emphasis, and Pedagogical Notes

- Use the linear/functions unit to review prior skills while deepening interpretation, units, intervals, and multiple representations.
- Treat quadratics as required content every semester: review prior methods, then deepen connections among forms, graphs, solutions, and models.
- Use the full variety of common quadratic forms rather than treating the unit only as equation solving.
- Emphasize conceptual understanding of domain, range, composition, and inverses; Math 49 revisits them at greater algebraic depth.
- In Math 31, composition should emphasize evaluation, units, meaning, and writing formulas more than decomposition or complex domain analysis.
- Connect concavity to increasing or decreasing average rates of change rather than calculus terminology alone.
- Watch for textbook quadratic problems that produce nonreal answers if complex numbers have not been reviewed.
- OpenStax is a resource mapped to the curriculum; it does not determine the objectives or unit order.
