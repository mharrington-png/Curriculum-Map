# Middlesex Math Course Overview

## MATH 21

**Course title:** Algebra and its Functions

**Status:** Draft normalized from the existing Math 21 course map; requires faculty review.

## 1. Review Content

Review content supports entry into Math 21. Teachers should diagnose and reinforce it as needed rather than treat it with the same pacing or assessment priority as new required content.

- Graphing lines and interpreting slope
- Common forms of linear equations
- Applications of linear functions
- Systems of linear equations as a diagnostic topic
- Basic exponent rules as needed before negative and fractional exponents

## 2. Required Content (Covered Every Semester)

### Unit 1: Functions and Function Interpretation

- Function notation and input/output
- Vertical line test
- Domain and range from graphs and tables
- Evaluating outputs and solving for inputs
- Interpretation of input and output in context
- Average rate of change

### Unit 2: Polynomials and Factoring

- Polynomial operations
- Greatest common factor
- Factoring trinomials
- Factoring special cases
- Connections between factors, zeros, and quadratic equations

### Unit 3: Rational Expressions and Equations

- Simplifying rational expressions with domain restrictions
- Multiplying and dividing rational expressions
- Adding and subtracting rational expressions
- Solving rational equations

### Unit 4: Exponents and Radicals

- Integer exponent rules
- Negative exponents
- Fractional exponents
- Radical notation and simplification
- Equivalence between radicals and rational exponents

### Unit 5: Quadratic Functions and Equations

- Quadratic functions in standard, factored, and vertex forms
- Factoring, square-root methods, completing the square, and the quadratic formula
- Graphical and algebraic interpretation of zeros and the vertex
- Projectile, area, and optimization applications

## 3. Extension Material (Optional/Time-Permitting)

- Complex numbers
- Additional depth and practice with exponents, radicals, and rational expressions

## 4. Depth, Emphasis, and Pedagogical Notes

- Coordinate Fall and Spring sections so both complete the same required content at comparable depth.
- Treat systems of equations as diagnostic exposure rather than a from-scratch unit.
- Teach functions with graphs, tables, formulas, and contexts; OpenStax requires supplementation here.
- When teaching units out of textbook order, screen exercises for assumed prior content, especially complex-number answers in quadratic work.
- Connect factoring techniques to quadratic graphs and solutions rather than presenting factoring only as symbolic manipulation.
- OpenStax is a resource mapped to the curriculum; it does not determine the objectives or unit order.
